Philosophical Origins of Spectral Ontology

From ancient Greek philosophy's notion of "being itself" to modern ontology, language philosophy, and logical realism, humanity has continuously questioned the foundation of existence.

The PAC–μ theory introduces a spectral ontology—a generative, dynamic model of being. It redefines existence as a compressible, activatable, and internally expressible spectral state rather than a static substance. This draws from diverse philosophical traditions:

  • Plato's idealism — structure as a prior model of truth
  • Aristotle's four causes — reinterpretation of formal and final causes
  • Spinoza's self-caused substance — ontology must explain itself
  • Heidegger's Dasein — being as appearance and unfolding
  • Topos-theoretic ontology — unity of logic, language, and truth

The central question posed is: What counts as expressible being? PAC–μ answers: that which is compressible under μ and expressible through internal logical structure.

Thus, spectral ontology offers not a metaphysical claim, but a generative, computable, and structurally coherent ontological framework.
